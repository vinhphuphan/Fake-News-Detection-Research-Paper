Fake News Detection in the Age of Large Language Models
Abstract
The proliferation of fake news in todays information age underscores the critical need for ro-
bust factuality checking methods. Large Language Models (LLMs) oer promising solutions
to this challenge. This study endeavors to reproduce the results of the FELM: Bench-
marking Factuality Evaluation of Large Language Models paper using the most recent
open-source LLMs such as Vicuna, Llama and Mistralai. Through replicating the FELM
benchmark, the study aim to assess the fact-checking capabilities of modern LLMs. Ad-
ditionally, the paper presents potential strategies for enhancing LLM performance on this
benchmark. The ultimate objective is to pave the way for a future where AI can accurately
support us in checking information and combating misinformation.
1 Introduction
The explosion of online social media platforms has transformed how people access information, but this
ease of access has come with a cost - the spread of fake news (Mitchell et al., 2016). Fake news refers to
inaccurate information presented as news in social media. For instance, during the COVID-19 pandemic
, the circulation of misinformation about drinking highly concentrated alcohol could sanitize the body
and eradicate the virus, resulted in over 5,800 hospitalizations (Islam et al., 2020). This brings about
devastating eects on human s lives. Thus, fact-checking has become a growing public concern and a
critical issue.
Traditional fact-checking methods struggle with the vast volume and rapid spread of misinformation
online. Large Language Models (LLMs) emerge as a potential game-changer. LLMs possess the remark-
able ability to eciently analyze massive amounts of text data which empowers faster and more scalable
fact-checking. However, before people can condently integrate LLMs into real-world fact-checking ap-
plications, a robust evaluation framework is crucial. A reliable benchmark can assess their accuracy and
factuality checking abilities. Establishing such a benchmark is a critical step in preparing for a future
where AI can be a trusted partner in information verication.
This research project addresses this need by replicating and analyzing the FELM: Benchmarking Fac-
tuality Evaluation of Large Language Models paper (Chen et al., 2023). FELM provides a standardized
framework for evaluating LLM performance in factuality checking tasks. By replicating FELM, this
study can assess the current capabilities of modern LLMs in this domain and identify areas for im-
provement. Furthermore, this work will explore potential ideas for enhancing LLM performance on the
FELM benchmark. While fully optimizing LLMs for real-world fact-checking applications may require
further research and development, this project contributes to the ongoing battle against misinformation
by evaluating their potential and paving the way for the development of reliable AI-powered fact-checking
tools.
2 Related Work
This section explores existing benchmarks for factuality detection, with a particular focus on FELM. Prior
factuality detection benchmarks have often been limited in scope, primarily focusing on specic tasks such
as summarization (Kryscinski et al., 2020) or specic knowledge domains veriable with evidence from
Wikipedia (Kamoi et al., 2023). In these benchmarks, factuality evaluation revolved around determining
whether a given text could be supported by relevant evidence. For instance, summarization factuality
detection assessed if the generated summary aligns with the original documents factual content. This
limitation meant that other benchmarks often required factuality detection methods to have an external
retrieval module to determine factuality.
1
2.1 FELM: Benchmarking Factuality Evaluation of Large Language Models
To address these limitations, the FELM: Benchmarking Factuality Evaluation of Large Language Mod-
els paper (Chen et al., 2023) introduced a comprehensive benchmark designed to evaluate the factuality
checking ability of Large Language Models (LLMs) across diverse domains. In its design principles, FELM
underscores the importance of assessing factual correctness in text generation systems, encompassing er-
rors such as incorrect entities, fabricated references, misleading scientic claims, illogical reasoning, and
mathematical inaccuracies. Unlike existing benchmarks that focus on single domains, FELM aims to
cover multiple distinct domains to reect the broad generalization capabilities of LLMs. The benchmark
adopts a user-oriented perspective, utilizing segment-level annotation to provide ne-grained evaluations
directly from model responses. Additionally, FELM aims to oer detailed error information, including
error type, reason, and reference links, to enhance interpretability and usefulness for users.
FELM comprises ve diverse domains: World Knowledge, Science and Technology, Recommendation and
Writing, Reasoning, and Math. Each domain encapsulates specic areas where factual errors may occur,
such as inaccuracies in knowledge about entities, scientic claims, recommendations, reasoning steps, and
mathematical problem-solving. The benchmark consists of a total of 817 samples and 3948 segments, en-
suring adequate coverage and representation across domains. The construction process involves gathering
prompts from various online platforms, standard benchmarks, and self-instructed ChatGPT generations,
ensuring a diverse and comprehensive dataset for evaluating LLM factuality across dierent tasks and
scenarios.
2.2 ReAct: Synergizing Reasoning and Acting in Language Models
Building on the concept of reasoning and acting separately in LLMs, Yao et al. (2022) introduced
the ReAct framework - a prompting method. This framework allows LLMs to generate reasoning traces
alongside performing task-specic actions in an iterative manner. These reasoning traces act as a thought
process for the LLM, enabling it to develop, track, and update plans for actions, even handling unexpected
situations. The action component empowers the LLM to interact with external sources such as knowledge
bases to gather information.
By incorporating interaction with external tools through ReAct, LLMs can retrieve additional informa-
tion, leading to more reliable and fact-checkable responses. Yao et al. found that ReAct outperformed
several leading models on language and decision-making tasks. Additionally, ReAct improved the inter-
pretability and trustworthiness of LLMs for humans.

3.3 Enhancing Performance
Initial experiments without external context highlighted the importance of contextual data in the veri-
cation process. To address this, I propose leveraging trustworthy external sources such as Wikipedia and

PolitiFact. This approach builds upon the ReAct framework (Yao et al., 2023), which allows LLMs to
interact with these sources during the fact-checking process.
Fact-checking inherently involves nding trustworthy sources to verify claims. Therefore, LLMs must
leverage external sources to support their fact-checking processes. The model is initially prompted with
a claim and decides whether to take actions such as retrieving information with a dierent query. If the
model fails to answer after few iterations, it provides a nal answer based on all previous searches.
When receiving the claim, the LLM rst decomposes the claim into segments for verication. The
agent then formulates queries based on each segment and interacts with external sources to verify their
accuracy. If any segment is unsupported by external sources, it indicates a potential error in the claim.
Conversely, if all segments are supported, the LLM returns a message conrming the claims correctness.

3.4 Trustworthy Sources
While search engines such as Google Search or Bing may seem to be natural choices for information
retrieval, they are not preferred sources for fact-checking in this context. These search engines index a
vast amount of web content, including user-generated content, forums, and blogs, where accuracy and
reliability can vary signicantly. As such, relying solely on these search engines for fact verication may
lead to unreliable results, as the information retrieved may not be reliable or trustworthy.
Instead, the fact-checking framework prioritizes sources such as Wikipedia, Wolfram, and PolitiFact,
which are known for their reliability and credibility in providing factual information. Wikipedia, for
instance, is a collaborative encyclopedia with strict editorial guidelines and a large community of editors
dedicated to maintaining accuracy. Wolfram oers structured and veried data across various domains,
while PolitiFact specializes in fact-checking political statements with rigorous methodologies.
3
By focusing on these trusted sources, the framework aims to ensure the accuracy and reliability of
the information used in the fact-checking process, thereby enhancing the credibility of our results and
contributing to the ght against misinformation.
In the verication process, the LLM interacts with various external sources to assess the factual accuracy
of claims.The sources utilized for verication include:
1. Wikipedia: A widely used online encyclopedia that provides comprehensive information on various
topics. It serves as a valuable resource for fact-checking and verifying claims.
2. Wolfram: A computational knowledge engine that provides accurate and reliable information on
various topics, including science, history, and more.
3. PolitiFact: A fact-checking website that rates the accuracy of claims made by politicians and other
public gures.
4. Snopes: A fact-checking website that debunks urban legends, myths, and misinformation.
5. ScienceDirect: A leading platform for scientic and technical journals and books.

This experiment focuses on the segment-based method for assessing LLM factuality judgments within

To explore dierent approaches within the segment-based method, the research will examine four varia-
tions:
1. Vanilla: The most basic approach where LLMs make factuality judgments solely based on the
provided question and the individual text segments.
2. Chain-of-Thought (CoT): LLMs are rst prompted to generate a thought process outlining their
reasoning ( Wei et al., 2022) before making the nal factuality prediction.
3. Reference Link: the experiment investigate if providing the reference links included in the FELM
dataset can improve LLM performance. These links often contain relevant information that can be
helpful for factuality assessment.
4. Reference Document: Building on the reference link concept, this variation involves retrieving
the actual text corresponding to the links. The system will then employ the BM25 algorithm
(Robertson et al., 2009) to extract the most relevant text snippets and provide them as additional
input to the LLMs.

5 Conclusion
This research investigated the performance of factual error detectors built on large language models
(LLMs), specically Vicuna, LLama, and Mistralai. The evaluation across various domains using F1
score, precision, recall, and balanced accuracy metrics revealed a nuanced performance landscape. Vicuna
initially led the evaluation, but LLama surpassed it after leveraging external information, demonstrating
improved factuality assessment abilities. Mistralai exhibited lower performance overall. The results show
that integrating trustworthy external sources signicantly enhanced the performance of all LLMs. LLama
emerged as the most procient LLM for fact-checking after enhancement, particularly excelling in the
Reasoning domain. When compared to the previous results from the FELM paper (Chen et al., 2023),
GPT-4 remains the top performer with an F1 score of 48.3 without external resources.
Despite the advancements showcased, this study has limitations, including the use of lightweight LLM
versions and a focus on segment-based methods. However, the integration of trustworthy external sources,
such as Wikipedia, marks a promising step towards enhancing fact-checking processes and addressing
misinformation complexities.
In conclusion, this study provides valuable insights into the capabilities and limitations of LLMs for
factuality assessment. While there are limitations to this study, including the use of lightweight LLMs
and the focus on segment-based evaluation, it contributes to the development of trustworthy and accurate
AI-powered fact-checking systems, crucial in todays information age. Future research can build upon
this work to further enhance the performance of LLMs and create more eective fact-checking tools.
